01 Problem

The AI worked.
The experience didn't.

Users were dropping off before reaching the core value of the product.
The issue wasn't the correction engine, it was everything around it.

Navigation was unclear, account management was fragmented, and the payment flow required users to move across multiple steps at the point where clarity and trust were most critical. Even the correction experience required unnecessary effort due to layout inefficiencies.

I worked as the sole designer, collaborating closely with a PM and developers to diagnose and redesign the core experience.

UI before redesign

The Data
Internal data showed that 58% of users who reached the pricing page
did not complete checkout. Indicating a significant gap between intent and conversion.
02 Research

Where were users losing
trust and effort?

Was drop-off caused by navigation confusion, structural friction,
or inefficiencies in the core writing experience?

Through user interviews, VoC review, and competitive benchmarking, I mapped where users were losing trust and effort.

User Interviews
Interviewed 15 active and inactive users to map where they lost confidence or abandoned flows.
Support Ticket Review
Review of recurring support tickets to surface patterns
in user confusion and unmet expectations.
Competitive Benchmarking
Benchmarking against similar AI writing tools to
identify where Pcanpi broke category conventions.
Finding 01
Conflicting mental models in payment flows
Users could upgrade through two paths: 'Account' → 'Plan' tab or /pricing page. These paths behaved differently, creating uncertainty about where the actual purchase happened. Users switched between both, unsure if they had completed payment, breaking trust at a critical moment.
Finding 02
Structural friction in account flows
Account actions (profile, subscription, billing) were split across multiple tabs without clear hierarchy. Users often checked multiple sections to complete a single task, increasing effort for high-intent actions. Support tickets reflected this confusion.
Finding 03
Category expectation mismatch
Competitors used single-page checkout with plan and payment visible together. Engram required multiple steps across screens, breaking expected patterns at the point of purchase.
Finding 04
Interaction friction in the core product
The editor used a vertical layout, text input at the top, suggestions below. Users needed to scroll frequently to compare content, increasing cognitive load and slowing the editing experience.
KEY INSIGHT
Users were blocked at two levels, reaching the product,
and using it efficiently once inside.
The problem was not visual, it was system-level friction across both flow and interaction. This required addressing navigation, payment, account structure, and the editor in a single coherent redesign.
03 Approach

Two problem spaces,
one system.

Given the one-month timeline, the redesign focused on
high-impact flows directly tied to conversion and first-use experience rather than full product coverage.

The work addressed both flow-level friction (navigation, account, payment) and interaction-level friction (writing experience).

Reusable components were introduced to prototype key flows quickly and support consistent implementation.

Key Changes
Removed competing upgrade flows to eliminate ambiguity at the point of conversion
Consolidated checkout into a single screen with plan selection and payment visible together
Simplified account structure into a clear hierarchy: Subscription → Usage → Billing → Profile → Security
Redesigned editor into a side-by-side layout to reduce scrolling and improve comparison during editing

04 Final Design

Designing for continuity
not navigation.

Four core experiences redesigned to let users complete tasks
without losing context or switching screens.

Writing Experience
The editor is the product.

Layout restructured into a side-by-side system (4:6 ratio), input on the left, corrections on the right.

Users can compare content and feedback without scrolling, reducing effort per edit.

06 Reflection

Reducing friction can have
more impact than adding features.

Improving the experience required addressing both how users reach the product, and how efficiently they use it once inside.

Instrument earlier

Pre-defined funnel tracking would have allowed more precise attribution of impact, connecting specific design decisions to specific drop-off points, rather than attributing gains to the redesign as a whole.

Use session recording tools

Using Hotjar and session recordings would have surfaced key friction points sooner, reducing reliance on VoC and enabling iteration before launch.

Design mobile-first

The editor and checkout were optimized for desktop first. The mobile experience remains less refined than intended, a limitation that became clearer only after launch.
